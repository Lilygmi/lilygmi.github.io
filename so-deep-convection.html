Southern Ocean Deep Convection

Understanding why the Southern Ocean matters for global carbon and how deep convection can change the way this region stores and releases CO2.

Why the Southern Ocean Matters

The Southern Ocean covers roughly 10% of the global ocean area but accounts for around 30–40% of the ocean’s anthropogenic CO₂ uptake, making it a dominant regulator of the climate system.

What is Deep Convection?

Deep convection is an episodic winter process that mixes cold, dense surface water down into the abyss and returns carbon-rich deep water to the surface, directly linking the deep ocean with air-sea CO₂ exchange.

Why is this Important

Southern Ocean carbon uptake is highly variable and poorly constrained in many models, especially when physical processes like deep convection and overturning circulation shift under climate change.

Project Overview

The goal of this project is to examine how deep convection events in the Southern Ocean shape its role in the global carbon cycle. We will combine physical oceanographic and biogeochemical analytics to identify the mechanisms driving short-term, seasonal, and long-term variability in ocean carbon content.

Longer description

The work explores variability in dissolved inorganic carbon and associated carbon fluxes during deep convection events in the Southern Ocean. It is designed to connect event-driven ventilation with regional carbon uptake and transport, using observational analyses and model-based diagnostics to improve understanding of how convective mixing influences air-sea carbon exchange.

Key research elements include event identification, carbon anomaly assessment, comparison with remote sensing and model output, and the development of narrative insights about how convective processes shape Southern Ocean carbon variability.
